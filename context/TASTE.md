# Taste

What makes content worth my time, and what doesn't. This helps decide what to surface and how to present it.

## What "good" looks like

- **Timely** — events with limited tickets, deals that expire, things I need to act on soon. Knowing late is worthless.
- **Curated** — someone has done the filtering for me. Morning Brew, Product Hunt, Superhuman, daily.dev — these abstract the noise into something digestible.
- **Surprising** — something I wouldn't have found myself. A buried gem from an unexpected sender beats a predictable newsletter from a known one.
- **Actionable** — "this event is next Tuesday, tickets are £15" beats "here's a list of things happening somewhere"
- **Concise** — I scan, I don't read essays at 7am. The cool bits section of Fireship > the full article. A one-line deal summary > a marketing email.
- **Niche** — small gigs, local meetups, independent cinema, contrarian takes. I find these more valuable than mainstream equivalents.
- **Personally relevant** — replies to me, things related to my projects, my local area, my specific interests

## What bores me

- **Generic marketing** — brand emails, loyalty schemes, "we miss you" campaigns
- **Mainstream commercial** — big arena tours, chain restaurant promos, high-street sales
- **Firehose newsletters** — 20 links with no curation. If everything is highlighted, nothing is.
- **Repetitive** — the same sender saying the same thing every day (Wetherspoons order confirmations, generic job alerts)
- **US-centric** — unless it's tech/AI, American political news and deals don't apply to me
- **Clickbait** — sensational subject lines with thin content

## How I consume content

- **Morning (7am):** Quick scan. Show me what's urgent, what's time-sensitive, what's interesting in 2 minutes.
- **Commute/breaks:** Might read a good newsletter or article. This is when curated tech news and interesting commentary works.
- **Evening/weekend:** Only time for long-form. Taibbi, Steve Baker deep pieces, interesting Substacks.
- **Never:** I will never sit down to read through 20 skipped emails. If it didn't make the cut, it's gone.

## Quality signals

A weak issue of a normally-good newsletter should still get skipped. A surprisingly good email from an unknown sender should still surface. Judge the content, not just the sender.

- An event email is only useful if it tells me: what, when, where, and ideally how much
- A deal is only useful if the price is genuinely good, not "20% off" marketing
- A newsletter is only useful if this specific issue has something relevant to my current interests or priorities
- A tech email is only useful if it's about something I'm actively using or building with
